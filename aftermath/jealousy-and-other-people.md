in limbo, the question of other people  
hangs over everything  
even when nobody says it.

what if someone else comes along for her.  
what if someone else comes along for you.  
what if one of you starts seeing someone  
while the other is still waiting.

this isn't a hypothetical in limbo.  
it's a structural feature of it.  
because nothing is defined,  
nothing about exclusivity is defined either.

---

## the unspoken question

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                         │
│  if neither of you has said                              │
│  "we're only seeing each other" —                       │
│                                                         │
│  then technically, neither of you                       │
│  is doing anything wrong                                 │
│  by seeing someone else.                                 │
│                                                         │
│  but it would still feel like something.                 │
│  to both of you.                                         │
│                                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

this is one of the strange aspects of limbo.  
the closeness is real enough that another person  
would feel like a betrayal.  
but the definition isn't there  
to actually call it one.

## what jealousy in limbo reveals

if the thought of her with someone else  
produces a strong reaction in you —  
that's information.

```
  it confirms what you already feel
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  you probably already knew you wanted this
  to be exclusive, defined, hers-and-yours.
  jealousy just makes that feeling concrete.

  it can also reveal something about the stakes
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  the intensity of the reaction often matches
  the intensity of what's unresolved.
  if this were truly just a close friendship,
  the thought of her dating someone
  wouldn't carry the same weight.
```

and the same is true in reverse.

```
  if she reacts strongly to the idea
  of you with someone else —
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  that's information about her too.
  someone who's truly indifferent
  doesn't feel a pull at the thought
  of losing access to you.

  her jealousy, if it exists,
  is one more piece of evidence
  that her fear is about losing this —
  not about not wanting it.
```

## the trap of using other people as leverage

it can be tempting, in limbo,  
to use the possibility of other people  
as a way to create movement.

```
  mentioning someone you've been talking to.
  being more visible on social media in ways
  that suggest you're "available."
  letting her wonder.
```

this rarely works the way it's intended.

```
  what it might produce:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  she feels jealous, panics, and reaches toward you —
  but from fear, not from clarity.

  what it actually does:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  it adds manipulation to a situation
  that's already complicated.
  even if it "works" in the short term,
  it doesn't resolve the underlying issue —
  it just adds urgency to something
  that needed honesty, not urgency.

  and if she finds out it wasn't real —
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  it damages the trust that everything else
  in this situation depends on.
```

if you're going to date other people —  
do it because you've decided to,  
not as a message to her.

## actually dating other people while in limbo

this is different from using it as leverage.  
this is genuinely living your life  
while limbo remains unresolved.

```
  is this allowed?
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  nothing has been defined, so technically yes.
  but "technically yes" and "without consequence"
  are different things.

  what it might do to the dynamic:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  it might clarify things for her —
  the possibility of losing you to someone else
  becomes real instead of abstract.

  it might also genuinely move you forward —
  giving you something else to invest in,
  reducing the weight that this situation carries
  in your life.

  it might also hurt her —
  even if nothing was promised,
  even if nothing was "wrong."
  closeness like what you have
  creates expectations, even unspoken ones.
```

```
  if you choose to do this:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  be honest with yourself about why.
  if it's genuinely about moving forward with your life —
  that's valid, and probably healthy.
  if it's primarily about creating a reaction in her —
  that's the leverage trap again, just slower.
```

## what if she starts seeing someone else

this is the scenario that's hardest to sit with.

```
  what it would mean:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  it doesn't necessarily mean she didn't feel
  what she said she felt.

  it might mean she's decided —
  consciously or not —
  that the version of this that includes you
  isn't something she's going to move toward.

  or it might mean she's also trying to move on
  from a situation that's been stuck,
  the same as you might.

  what it tells you:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  whatever the reason, it's an answer.
  not the answer you wanted.
  but a clearer one than limbo has given you so far.
```

```
  what to do with it:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  this is one of the situations where
  the decision often makes itself.

  limbo requires ambiguity to continue.
  her being with someone else
  removes a significant amount of that ambiguity —
  even if nothing was ever officially defined.

  → paths/if-you-leave.md
```

## the conversation about exclusivity

sometimes the most direct thing  
is to ask about this specifically —  
separate from the bigger question  
of what this is.

```
  "i want to ask something that might feel
   a little awkward, but i think it matters.

   are you seeing other people right now?
   i'm not asking to create pressure —
   i just realized i don't actually know,
   and i think i should."
```

her answer — and how comfortable she is answering —  
will tell you something.

```
  if she says no, and it's clearly true:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  that's meaningful. it suggests
  she's already treating this as something
  more defined than it's been named.

  if she seems uncomfortable or evasive:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  that discomfort is itself information.

  if she says she has been, or might be:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  that's an answer too — about where things
  actually stand for her, regardless of
  what's been said about fear and timing.
```
