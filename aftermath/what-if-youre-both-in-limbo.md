---

most of this framework is written as if  
you're the one waiting  
and she's the one who's stuck.

but that might not be the whole picture.

what if she's not the only one  
who isn't moving?

what if you're also waiting —  
for her to be ready, for the timing to be right,  
for proof that this will work —  
and neither of you has actually moved  
toward the other?

---

## the version of limbo where both people are stuck

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                         │
│  she's stuck because she's scared of losing             │
│  what exists by trying for more.                        │
│                                                         │
│  you might be stuck because you're waiting              │
│  for her to move first —                                │
│  for her to be the one who decides,                     │
│  who initiates the change,                              │
│  who takes the risk.                                     │
│                                                         │
│  two people, both waiting,                              │
│  both for the other to move.                            │
│                                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

this isn't a criticism.  
it's just often true,  
and worth being honest about.

---

## how this shows up

```
  you've said what you want — once
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  and since then, you've been waiting
  for her response to evolve.
  you haven't said it again. you haven't pushed.
  you've been patient — which is good —
  but patience can also be its own form of waiting
  for her to do the work.

  you're staying in the current dynamic
  because leaving feels too final
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  not because you believe staying will work,
  but because you're not ready to be the one
  who ends it.

  you've been hoping time will resolve it
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  same as her. "give it time" isn't only
  something she said to you.
  it might be something you're also telling yourself.

  you haven't asked the direct question
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  because asking it risks an answer
  you're not ready to hear.
  not asking protects the possibility —
  for both of you.
```

---

## why this matters

if you're both stuck, the dynamic isn't  
"she won't move and i'm waiting."

it's "neither of us is moving,  
and we're both telling ourselves  
we're waiting for the other."

```
  this changes the framing:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  it's not about whether she'll eventually choose.
  it's about whether either of you will be the one
  to actually change something —
  the conversation, the dynamic, the waiting itself.

  someone has to move first.
  if you're waiting for her to move first,
  and she's scared to move at all —
  the situation can stay exactly as it is
  indefinitely. not because neither of you wants more.
  because neither of you is willing to be
  the one who changes the shape of things.
```

---

## what "moving first" could look like — for you

this isn't about repeating the confession.

```
  it could mean:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  initiating the direct question
  (harder/does-she-know-what-she-wants.md)
  instead of waiting for her to bring clarity.

  naming the cost to you — out loud —
  instead of carrying it quietly
  and hoping she notices.

  making an actual decision about your own limit
  instead of waiting indefinitely
  for her fear to resolve on its own.

  choosing to leave — not as a tactic,
  but because you've decided
  that waiting isn't something you're willing
  to keep doing without direction.
```

---

## the uncomfortable possibility

if you're both stuck —  
it's possible that part of what's keeping this in place  
isn't just her fear.

it's also the comfort, for both of you,  
of not having to risk anything more  
than what's already been risked.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                         │
│  she risked telling you her fear.                       │
│  you risked telling her what you want.                  │
│                                                         │
│  both of those were real risks.                         │
│  both of you took them.                                 │
│                                                         │
│  but maybe both of you have also,                       │
│  since then, been protecting yourselves                 │
│  from the next risk —                                   │
│  the one that would actually change something.          │
│                                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

---

## what to do if this is true

```
  acknowledge it — to yourself first
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  not as self-blame.
  just as honest information.
  "i've also been waiting for her to move."

  decide what moving first looks like for you
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  it doesn't have to be dramatic.
  it could be the direct question.
  it could be naming a timeline to yourself.
  it could be deciding you'll have one more
  real conversation, and basing your next steps
  on what happens in it.

  don't wait for her to move first anymore
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  if you've been waiting for her —
  and she's been waiting too —
  someone has to break the symmetry.

  it might as well be the person
  who's been carrying more of the cost.
  that's you.
```
