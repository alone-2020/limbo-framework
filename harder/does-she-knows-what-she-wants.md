---

this is the question underneath everything.

because the answer changes what you do.

if she's scared but wants this —  
patience and consistency make sense.  
if she doesn't know what she wants —  
time might eventually produce clarity.  
if she knows she doesn't want it  
but can't bring herself to say so —  
staying is just pain deferred.

---

## the three possibilities

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                         │
│  1.  she wants this and is scared of losing it          │
│      by trying                                          │
│                                                         │
│  2.  she genuinely doesn't know yet                     │
│      and needs time to find out                         │
│                                                         │
│  3.  she knows she doesn't want it                      │
│      but doesn't want to lose what you have             │
│      by saying so                                       │
│                                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

most people in limbo assume they're in scenario 1.  
scenario 3 exists more than people want to admit.  
scenario 2 is real but has a time limit.

---

## signals she wants this and is scared

```
  she initiates at the same level you do or more
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  someone who doesn't want something
  doesn't invest energy in maintaining it.
  consistent initiation from her side is real signal.

  she's physically present when she can be
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  she's come to you. twice.
  people go where they want to be.

  she's said things that imply a future
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  "if something can be built here, that's okay."
  that's not the language of someone who doesn't want it.
  that's the language of someone who does
  and is trying to leave the door open
  while managing their fear.

  she told you that you wouldn't disappoint her
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  this is significant.
  it means she's thought about it clearly enough
  to have an answer to your concern.
  that's not a casual response.

  her fear is about loss, not about you
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  she's afraid of losing what you have.
  not afraid of you specifically.
  not avoiding you.
  this distinction matters.
```

---

## signals she genuinely doesn't know

```
  her answers change slightly over time
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  not dramatically — but she's clearly processing.
  she's not settled in her position.
  she's working something out.

  she asks questions about what it would look like
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  curious rather than closed.
  "what would that actually mean for us?"
  this is someone exploring, not avoiding.

  she acknowledges the unfairness to you
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  "i know this isn't fair to you."
  "i'm sorry i can't be clearer."
  this shows she's aware of the cost — which means
  she's thinking about it honestly, not deflecting.
```

## signals she knows but can't say it

this is the hardest one to read.  
because the behavior can look like fear  
when it's actually something else.

```
  the reasons don't evolve
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  every conversation lands in the same place.
  the fear is the same size it was six months ago.
  nothing has shifted despite your consistency.
  fear usually responds to safety over time.
  if it doesn't — it might not be fear.

  she never reaches toward more
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  you're the one who raised it.
  you're the one who brings it back.
  she responds — but she never initiates
  the conversation about the future herself.

  she pivots away from clarity
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  not to something else — just away from the topic.
  every direct question gets redirected
  back to how good things are now.
  which is true. and also incomplete.

  the comfort of the current state is her answer
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  what she has now — the closeness, the daily contact,
  your investment — without the commitment.
  if she has all of that without naming it,
  there may not be enough motivation
  to take the risk of naming it.
```

---

## the direct question

if you've been in limbo long enough —  
and you need to know —  
there is one honest question you can ask.

not "do you want to be with me."  
that puts too much pressure on a single answer.

instead:

```
  "i need to ask you something honestly,
   and i need you to be honest back —
   even if the answer is hard.

   do you want this to become something?
   not now, not with a deadline —
   but in the future, is that something you want?

   because i need to know if i'm waiting for something
   that's possible for you
   or if i'm waiting for something that isn't."
```

this question does several things:

```
  it removes the pressure of "right now"
  it gives her room to say yes or no honestly
  it frames your wait as finite and real
  it makes your need visible without being an ultimatum
  it invites truth rather than comfort
```

her answer — and how she answers —  
will tell you more than months of observation.

---

## reading the answer

```
  "yes, i want that, i'm just scared."
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  this is the clearest positive answer.
  take it seriously. assess what she does after.
  see if behavior matches the answer.

  "i don't know."
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  honest and incomplete.
  ask what would help her know.
  is there something specific, or is it open-ended?
  "i don't know" with a path is different from
  "i don't know" with no direction.

  "i don't want to lose what we have."
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  this is not an answer to the question.
  it's a redirection to safety.
  gently name this:
  "i understand that. but what i asked was
   whether you want it to become something —
   not whether you're afraid of losing it."

  "no."
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  this is rare but it's the clearest answer.
  believe it. read paths/if-you-leave.md.
```
