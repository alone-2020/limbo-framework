---

the first problem with limbo is that it has no name.

and things without names are harder to think about clearly.  
you can't describe it to someone else without it sounding  
either more serious or less serious than it actually is.

so before anything else — let's name it.

---

## what limbo is

limbo is when two people have built something real —  
real closeness, real care, real daily presence —  
but have not agreed on what that thing is  
or where it's going.

it is not:

```
  a friendship with feelings on one side
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  in limbo, both people feel something.
  the feelings are mutual. the uncertainty is mutual.
  what's missing is not feeling — it's decision.

  a situation where one person said no
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  no one said no here.
  what was said is closer to:
  "i want this but i'm scared of what happens
   if we try and it breaks."

  a situation that needs more time to develop
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  what you have is already developed.
  the intimacy, the closeness, the daily contact —
  those exist. what's missing is not more connection.
  it's the courage to name what the connection already is.
```

---

## what limbo looks like from the outside

if someone looked at what you have with this person,  
they would call it a relationship.

```
  daily contact                     ✓
  emotional intimacy                ✓
  physical presence when possible   ✓
  shared activities and accounts    ✓
  gifts and acts of care            ✓
  she initiates                     ✓
  you've met in person               ✓
  you've talked about feelings       ✓
```

the only thing missing is the word.  
and the agreement about what comes next.

---

## why the word matters

it might seem like a small thing.  
it isn't.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                         │
│  without the word, there is no agreement.               │
│  without the agreement, there is no security.           │
│  without security, everything you've built              │
│  rests on an understanding that was never confirmed.    │
│                                                         │
│  the word isn't just a label.                           │
│  it's a commitment to what you're doing.                │
│                                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

and right now, only one of you is fully committed.  
or rather — both of you are committed to the connection,  
but only one of you is willing to say what the connection is.

---

## the specific pain of limbo

limbo produces a specific kind of pain  
that's different from rejection and different from uncertainty.

```
  it's not the pain of not having something
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  you have a lot. daily presence, intimacy, care.
  the pain isn't absence.

  it's the pain of having something real
  that exists in an undefined state
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  you can't fully invest because it's not confirmed.
  you can't fully let go because it's too real.
  you're neither in nor out.
  and that position, held for long enough,
  becomes its own kind of exhausting.

  it's the pain of wanting to build something
  and being held at the threshold
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  you can see the door.
  you're standing right in front of it.
  and the person you want to walk through it with
  is standing next to you, holding your hand,
  and not moving.
```

---

## the illusion of stability

limbo often feels stable.  
things are good. connection is strong. she's there.

but what feels like stability is actually stasis.

```
  stability:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  a foundation that things can be built on.
  something secure enough to grow from.

  stasis:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  nothing is getting worse.
  but nothing is moving either.
  it looks like stability from the outside.
  it feels like being frozen from the inside.
```

stasis is comfortable for the person  
who doesn't need it to move.  
it's slowly depleting for the person who does.

---

## who limbo serves

this is uncomfortable but worth being honest about.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                                                         │
│  limbo, in its current form,                            │
│  serves her more than it serves you.                    │
│                                                         │
│  she has the closeness. she has the daily contact.      │
│  she has someone who cares for her deeply.              │
│  and she doesn't have to take the risk                  │
│  of naming what it is.                                  │
│                                                         │
│  you have the closeness too.                            │
│  but you also have the cost of wanting more             │
│  and not having it confirmed.                           │
│                                                         │
│  that's not a balanced position.                        │
│                                                         │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

this doesn't make her a bad person.  
it makes her human — holding on to something good  
because she's afraid of losing it.

but it means the current situation  
is not sustainable for you indefinitely.

---

## the thing about "give it time"

time is not a plan.

```
  time helps when:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  something specific needs to change first
  (circumstances, distance, life situation)
  and both people know what they're waiting for.

  time doesn't help when:
  ──────────────────────────────────────────
  what's in the way is fear —
  because fear doesn't resolve by waiting.
  it resolves by deciding.
```

if she's scared of losing what you have —  
more time in the current dynamic  
gives her more of what she's afraid to lose.  
it doesn't reduce the fear.  
it increases what's at stake.
